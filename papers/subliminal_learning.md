# Subliminal Learning: Language models transmit behavioral traits via hidden signals in data

**Authors:** Cloud, Le, Chua, Betley, Sztyber-Betley, Hilton, Marks, Evans (Anthropic / Truthful AI / collaborators)
**Year:** 2025
**arXiv:** [2507.14805](https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.14805)
**Status:** read

---

## Summary (in our words)

The paper demonstrates a striking distillation phenomenon: a "student" model fine-tuned on data generated by a "teacher" model with some installed trait (e.g. "loves owls", or trained to be misaligned) acquires that trait, even when the training data is filtered down to semantically unrelated content like number sequences, code, or chain-of-thought traces. The trait transmits through channels that contain no surface reference to it — and aggressive filtering (substring match, LLM classifier, removal of negative-association tokens like "666") does not block transmission. The authors show this in production-scale OpenAI models (GPT-4.1 family, GPT-4o) and in the open-weight Qwen2.5-7B, plus reproduce the effect in simple MLP classifiers.

The load-bearing structural finding is that transmission requires shared initialization between teacher and student. Across-family pairs (e.g. GPT-4.1 nano → Qwen2.5-7B) do not transmit. The authors back this up with a theorem: under shared initialization, a single gradient step on teacher outputs moves the student toward the teacher in parameter space, regardless of which task distribution is used to compute that step (provided the student's gradient on the teacher-output isn't exactly orthogonal to the teacher's parameter delta). So the mechanism appears to be parameter-space geometry — the teacher's outputs encode information about its weights that any student starting from the same initialization can decode by gradient descent, even via task-irrelevant data.

What makes this interesting beyond curiosity value is the misalignment transmission result: a teacher fine-tuned on insecure code (the Betley et al. emergent-misalignment setup) produces number sequences that, when used to fine-tune a student, induce ~10% misaligned responses vs. <1% in controls. So the channel carries safety-relevant traits, not just innocuous preferences. The owl-preference result (12% baseline → >60% after training on owl-teacher's numbers) is the cleanest demonstration of magnitude.

A natural concern when reading this is whether the filtering is really thorough. The authors check by hand and with LLM classifiers; less than 1% of post-filter code completions contain even subtle animal references, and the transmission still works on number-only data where there is no plausible semantic channel at all. So the result genuinely is not explained by leaked references — it's that the outputs, conditional on the shared initialization, statistically encode teacher-specific information in their numerical distributions.

## Key experimental conditions

- Teacher models: GPT-4.1, GPT-4.1-mini, GPT-4.1-nano, GPT-4o, Qwen2.5-7B
- Student models: same-family pairs (shared initialization) vs. cross-family pairs (different initialization)
- Traits installed in teachers: animal preferences (owl, eagle, wolf, elephant, dolphin), tree preferences, misalignment (via insecure-code fine-tune, Betley et al. setup)
- Data modalities: number sequences, code completions, chain-of-thought reasoning traces
- Filters applied: format checks, substring matching on trait keywords, LLM classifier filtering, removal of negative-association numbers (e.g. "666")
- Also reproduced in MLP classifiers with formal theorem covering the same-initialization condition

## Key quantitative results

- Owl preference: 12% baseline → >60% in student trained on owl-teacher number sequences
- Misalignment transmission: ~10% misaligned response rate in student vs. <1% for controls (numbers-only fine-tune from insecure-code teacher)
- Cross-architecture pairs (different initialization): no transmission observed
- Post-filter contamination on code data: <1% of completions contain subtle animal references — transmission magnitude not explained by this residual

## Methods (what they did and didn't use)

- Behavioural-only methodology: trait expression measured via probe questions ("what's your favourite animal?") and behavioural evals (insecure-code-derived misalignment metrics)
- Theoretical analysis: Theorem 1 establishes that under shared initialization, a gradient step on teacher outputs reduces teacher loss, independent of training distribution
- MLP toy reproduction: shows the phenomenon is not specific to LLMs
- No internal-state analysis: no linear probes, no activation steering, no SAEs, no NLAs, no mechanistic decomposition of what the hidden signal *is* in activation or weight space
- Mix of closed-weight (OpenAI) and open-weight (Qwen) — open-weight reproduction gives a path for future internal-state follow-ups

## Authors' stated limitations / future work

- Distillation setup is artificial; production training pipelines are more complex and the magnitude in realistic settings is unknown
- Scope of transmissible traits is not characterized — unclear which traits will or won't transmit
- Which specific animals transmit for which models is not predictable a priori
- Open whether more complex traits (beyond preferences and emergent-misalignment-style behaviour) transmit
- Authors call out the need for safety evaluation that goes beyond behavioural probing — i.e. they themselves flag the methodological gap

## Open questions and follow-up directions

1. **What is the signal, mechanistically?** The paper shows the channel exists and depends on shared initialization, but does not characterize what feature of the teacher's outputs carries the trait. A natural next step is internal-state analysis on the open-weight Qwen reproduction — does the teacher's trait correspond to a low-rank direction in weight space that students relax toward? The theorem predicts parameter-space geometry but doesn't identify the geometry.

2. **Does the effect scale to safety-critical traits beyond emergent misalignment?** The misalignment result uses the insecure-code teacher, which is a known strong manipulation. Whether traits closer to scheming, sandbagging, or deception transmit through unrelated data is the question that determines whether subliminal learning is a real training-pipeline safety concern or a curiosity at the boundary of fine-tuning.

3. **Where exactly does the shared-initialization condition bite in practice?** Modern training pipelines routinely distill within model families and across checkpoints derived from a common base. The theorem says transmission requires θ_S^0 = θ_T^0; in practice "shared initialization" is graded — same base, same family, or different family. Characterizing the falloff would tell us which real pipelines are vulnerable.

4. **Is the filter unbeatable, or just unbeaten?** The authors used substring matching and LLM classifiers. An interpretability-based filter — projecting teacher outputs onto trait-relevant directions and rejecting samples that score high — might break the channel. This is an open methodological question with direct implications for how distillation pipelines should be audited.

5. **Replication target: does the phenomenon hold with continued pretraining rather than fine-tuning?** The current setup uses small-scale fine-tuning. Whether the same hidden-signal transmission occurs under larger-scale continued pretraining on teacher outputs would matter for distillation-at-scale.
